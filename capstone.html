SENIOR CAPSTONE

A senior-level capstone project in Human Factors Product Design. Developing an educational tool for elementary school students through iterative design based in the human-centered approach.

Product Design, User-Centered Research

Exploring the powerful partnership between plants and technology.

Bridging the gap.

When tasked with designing a project within the theme of "the classroom of the future", I chose to step away from virtual reality and robots and focus on enhancing that which already exists in classrooms today. The research outlining the beneficial presence of plants in our daily life,

especially in educational settings, is undeniable. Embracing these principles, this project strived to develop a planter + app system to compliment STEM education with hands-on, plant-based projects tailored for late elementary school students.

Preliminary Research

Understanding my users.

Looking at the literature.

A literature review gave insight into using technology as an educational tool and environmental education for younger populations. The articles helped define creating a simple digital interface, providing students with authority as a way to promote user investment, and integrating opportunities for hands-on activities as some of the most important factors to help motivate my designs.

A word from teachers.

Understanding elementary school teachersâ€™ perspectives was vital to developing a successful design. A survey was sent to school teachers in three different states. The survey was designed to provide insight into available resources and teaching techniques, which highlighted limiting the physical space requirement of the final product as an important factor to drive my designs.

Asking the experts.

Expert interviews with environmental educators, professors in cognitive development, and education specialists addressed the project through a variety of lenses and helped to better define the scope. Some of the most critical takeaways that would motivate the designs as I moved forward included designing something that complimented present day classrooms and learning objectives and embracing collaboration.

Understanding the market.

A competitive analysis looked at related products on the market today. Three existing smart connected planters were analyzed in terms of their intended use cases, included technologies, accessibility (specifically in terms of size and price), and customer opinions. In addition to being a valuable source of inspiration as I stepped forward with concept design, this analysis highlighted what the market presently lacked.

Rough concepts

From my initial user research, I created a list of six factors to consider when developing my initial concepts; physical space, collaboration, hands-on activity, user authority and investment, a simple digital interface, and a complement of fifth grade learning objectives. Following these factors and system requirements drawn from them, I created initial concepts for both the physical planter and the app- going for quantity and variety in designs.

ASKING MY USERS

An initial usability assessment was conducted with teachers and experts, utilizing both demo + reaction and expert reviews to collect feedback on the initial concepts I had created. From this feedback I was able to develop a list of 20+ system requirements that would guide the design of a single, testable prototype.
