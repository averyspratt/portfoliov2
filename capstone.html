SENIOR CAPSTONE

A senior-level capstone project in Human Factors Product Design. Developing an educational tool for elementary school students through iterative design based in the human-centered approach.

Product Design, User-Centered Research

Exploring the powerful partnership between plants and technology.

Bridging the gap.

When tasked with designing a project within the theme of "the classroom of the future", I chose to step away from virtual reality and robots and focus on enhancing that which already exists in classrooms today. The research outlining the beneficial presence of plants in our daily life,

especially in educational settings, is undeniable. Embracing these principles, this project strived to develop a planter + app system to compliment STEM education with hands-on, plant-based projects tailored for late elementary school students.

Preliminary Research

Understanding my users.

Looking at the literature.

A literature review gave insight into using technology as an educational tool and environmental education for younger populations.

A word from teachers.

Understanding elementary school teachers' perspectives was vital to developing a successful design - a survey sent to teachers in three different states helped get me there.

Asking the experts.

Expert interviews with environmental educators, professors in cognitive development, and education specialists addressed the project through a variety of lenses and helped to better define the scope.

Understanding the market.

A competitive analysis looked at related products on the market today. Three existing smart connected planters were analyzed in terms of their intended use cases, included technologies, accessibility, and customer opinions.

Rough concepts

From my initial user research, I created a list of six factors to consider when developing my initial concepts; physical space, collaboration, hands-on activity, user authority and investment, a simple digital interface, and a compliment of fifth grade learning objectives. Following these factors and system requirements drawn from them, I created initial concepts for both the physical planter and the app- going for quantity and variety in designs.

ASKING MY USERS

An initial usability assessment was conducted with teachers and experts, utilizing both demo + reaction and expert reviews to collect feedback on the initial concepts I had created. From this feedback I was able to develop a list of 20+ system requirements that would guide the design of a single, testable prototype.

View Usability Assessment #1

CREATING A PROTOTYPE

From my user feedback, I refined the designs of both the planter and web-app. Getting to a final testable planter prototype took three iterations (and a lot of carboard). The web app was created first by developing a user task-flow, and then creating a fully functional web-based prototype using the Adobe Create Suite, Sketch, and Proto.IO. These finalized protoypes were then put to the test in a stage-based testing session with late elementary school students.

PUTTING IT TO THE TEST

LOOKING AT THE BIG PICTURE

Testing with students confirmed that plants are an exciting pathway to STEM education.

UNCOVERING SOME UNEXPECTED GEMS

Seeds posed the risk of being boring and "science you cannot seeds." However, seeds are a cheaper alternative to starter plants and the students seemed more excited by the prospect of growing their own plants from seeds. Another unexpected hit were the plastic syringes - using these was an engaging (and afforadable) way to learn about precise measurements.
